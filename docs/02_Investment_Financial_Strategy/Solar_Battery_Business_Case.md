# Solar Battery Business Case

## Executive Summary

Stage 1 should be assessed as a 50 kW solar and 100 kWh battery project, designed so the site can later expand toward 100 kW solar.

The current financial baseline is the known electricity bill of approximately $1,200/month, or $14,400/year. The supporting usage baseline is approximately 93 kWh/day average. Because workshop activity is likely uneven, interval data is required before final sizing or finance approval.

At the current bill level, the project becomes attractive if it can reliably reduce the bill by 80-95%, if net installed cost stays near the $60,000-$80,000 target range, and if finance costs do not erase the benefit of preserving cash in the loan offset.

## Current Energy Cost Baseline

| Item | Current baseline |
| --- | ---: |
| Average monthly electricity bill | $1,200 |
| Estimated annual electricity bill | $14,400 |
| Average daily consumption | 93 kWh/day |
| Likely workday usage | Higher than average |

The bill is the primary business-case baseline because it captures both consumption and tariff structure. The 93 kWh/day figure is useful for system sizing, but it does not show peak demand, daytime load, export limits, demand charges, or machine start-up loads.

## Stage 1 System Assumptions

| Item | Stage 1 assumption |
| --- | ---: |
| Solar size | 50 kW |
| Battery size | 100 kWh |
| Future solar design target | 100 kW |
| Target net installed cost low | $60,000 |
| Target net installed cost high | $80,000 |
| Expansion allowance | Design electrical pathway for 100 kW solar |

The Stage 1 system should be quoted as a practical first deployment, not as a dead-end installation. Quotes should show the cost and design implications of later expansion.

## Conservative Solar Production Assumptions

For planning, 100 kW of panels should be treated as a reliable 40-60 kW daytime source, not as 100 kW continuous output.

This matters for workshop scheduling, EV charging, dam pumping, and battery charging. The model should not assume perfect output or perfect self-consumption.

## Bill Reduction Scenarios

| Bill reduction | Annual saving | Residual annual bill |
| --- | ---: | ---: |
| 70% | $10,080 | $4,320 |
| 80% | $11,520 | $2,880 |
| 90% | $12,960 | $1,440 |
| 95% | $13,680 | $720 |

The 80% case is a reasonable base case until interval data and quotes are available. The 90-95% cases may be possible if daytime load, battery control, tariff structure, and generator integration are favourable.

## Cash Purchase Scenario

Cash purchase avoids finance costs and keeps the project simple. It makes most sense when:

- Net installed cost is close to $60,000-$70,000.
- The system can reliably save at least $11,500/year.
- The after-tax benefit beats the return from leaving the same cash in the home loan offset.
- Depreciation or other tax treatment improves the effective return.

The main downside is opportunity cost. Cash used for the project no longer reduces home loan interest through the offset.

## Green Finance Scenario

Green finance may make sense if it preserves cash in the offset and the repayment is covered by energy savings.

Finance should be investigated across:

- Interest-free or low-interest government-backed options
- CEFC-backed green finance
- Bank green business loans
- Solar retailer finance
- Equipment finance
- Chattel mortgage
- Standard business loan

The business case should compare finance payments against the 70%, 80%, 90%, and 95% savings cases. Finance fees, early repayment terms, security, GST, depreciation, and tax deductibility need accountant review.

## Offset Account Opportunity Cost

The offset account benchmark is the avoided home loan interest. If the offset rate is 6%, using $70,000 of cash has an annual opportunity cost of about $4,200.

At an 80% bill reduction, annual savings are approximately $11,520. Before tax and maintenance, this leaves a gross annual benefit of about $7,320 after giving up a 6% offset return on $70,000.

## Waiting 12-24 Months Scenario

Waiting can be sensible if it allows better data and avoids a poor system design. However, waiting also means continuing to pay around $14,400/year in electricity costs.

Waiting makes sense when:

- Interval data is not yet available.
- Current quotes are above the target range.
- Finance rates are unattractive.
- Battery incentives or equipment prices may improve.
- Site electrical works or roof/ground locations are not yet clear.

Waiting is less attractive if a good quote, good finance, and clear load profile are available now.

## Key Risks

- Actual self-consumption may be lower than modelled.
- Demand charges may reduce apparent savings.
- Export limits may reduce solar value.
- Battery inverter capacity may constrain performance.
- Battery replacement cost is not yet included.
- Maintenance allowance is still a placeholder.
- Tax and depreciation treatment is not yet confirmed.
- Finance fees may make headline rates misleading.
- Future dam pump, EV charging, and workshop load growth may change the optimal design.

## Data Still Required

- Latest 12 electricity bills
- 12 months of interval data from the retailer
- Current tariff type and demand charge structure
- Major machine load list
- Roof and ground-mount solar map
- Export limit and network requirements
- Three commercial solar/battery quotes
- STC and battery incentive treatment shown separately
- Green finance and equipment finance options
- Accountant advice on depreciation, GST, tax deductibility, and R&D

## Recommendation

Proceed to a Stage 1 business-case validation round.

The preferred next step is not immediate purchase. It is to collect interval data, obtain three commercial quotes for 50 kW solar + 100 kWh battery, require each supplier to show the expansion pathway to 100 kW solar, and compare cash purchase against green finance and the home loan offset benchmark.

The project should move toward approval if quotes land near the $60,000-$80,000 target range, the system is likely to reduce the bill by at least 80%, and finance or tax treatment improves the outcome compared with leaving cash in offset.
