# Offset vs Solar Decision

## Purpose

This note explains how to decide between paying cash for solar and battery, using finance, or leaving cash in the home loan offset.

The current baseline is a $1,200/month electricity bill, or $14,400/year. The supporting usage baseline is 93 kWh/day average.

## When Paying Cash Makes Sense

Paying cash makes sense when the project return is clearly better than the offset return after allowing for risk, maintenance, tax, and future battery replacement.

Practical indicators:

- Net installed cost is near $60,000-$70,000.
- Annual savings are at least $11,500/year.
- Simple payback is around 5-6 years.
- The system design allows later expansion to 100 kW solar.
- Accountant confirms favourable depreciation or tax treatment.
- Cash reserves remain adequate after purchase.

## When Finance Makes Sense

Finance makes sense when it preserves cash in the offset and the annual repayment is lower than the annual energy saving.

Finance is especially attractive if:

- The rate is interest-free or genuinely low-interest.
- The loan term matches the useful life of the system.
- Fees are low.
- Early repayment is allowed.
- The business can deduct interest or depreciation, subject to accountant advice.
- Monthly savings broadly cover repayments.

## When Leaving Cash In The Offset Makes Sense

Leaving cash in the offset makes sense when the solar/battery project is uncertain or weak compared with the avoided home loan interest.

This is the better choice when:

- Quotes are above the target cost range.
- The likely bill reduction is below 70-80%.
- Finance rates are high.
- Interval data is missing.
- Export limits or demand charges undermine the savings case.
- Tax treatment is unclear.
- Cash is needed for higher-priority property or business uses.

## What Finance Rate Would Beat The Offset?

As a rule of thumb, finance becomes more attractive when the finance rate is below the offset rate, because the project can preserve cash that continues reducing home loan interest.

If the offset benchmark is 6%, finance below 6% deserves investigation. Finance above 6% may still work if tax treatment is favourable or cash preservation is important, but it must be tested carefully.

The true comparison should include fees, security, tax deductibility, repayment term, early repayment rules, and whether the financed system creates positive monthly cash flow.

## What Project Cost Makes The Deal Attractive?

At the current $14,400/year bill, the rough annual savings range is:

| Bill reduction | Annual saving |
| --- | ---: |
| 70% | $10,080 |
| 80% | $11,520 |
| 90% | $12,960 |
| 95% | $13,680 |

For a 5-year simple payback, the attractive project cost is approximately:

| Bill reduction | Maximum project cost for 5-year payback |
| --- | ---: |
| 70% | $50,400 |
| 80% | $57,600 |
| 90% | $64,800 |
| 95% | $68,400 |

This suggests that a $60,000 project can be attractive if bill reduction is around 85-90% or if tax treatment improves the result. A $70,000-$80,000 project needs stronger savings, better finance, or tax benefits to be compelling.

## What Annual Savings Are Needed For A 5-Year Payback?

| Project cost | Annual saving needed |
| --- | ---: |
| $60,000 | $12,000 |
| $70,000 | $14,000 |
| $80,000 | $16,000 |
| $100,000 | $20,000 |

The current bill is $14,400/year, so a 5-year payback becomes difficult above $70,000 unless the model includes tax benefits, higher future electricity prices, avoided demand charges, new loads, or other operational value.

## Working Decision Rule

Use this rule until better data is available:

- Prefer cash if net cost is low, savings are high, and the offset comparison remains positive.
- Prefer finance if the rate is below the offset rate and annual repayments are covered by savings.
- Prefer waiting if quotes are expensive, data is incomplete, or the tariff/load profile is unclear.
