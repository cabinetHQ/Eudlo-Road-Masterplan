# Grid vs Offgrid Strategy

## Executive Position

Stay grid-connected initially.

The system should be designed to become microgrid-capable, but full off-grid operation should only be considered later if real-world data proves it is operationally safe and financially sensible.

## Why Staying Grid-Connected Initially Is Recommended

The property has workshop and future manufacturing loads that may be costly to interrupt. The exact load profile is not yet known, and future loads are still developing.

Staying grid-connected initially provides:

- Backup capacity during low solar periods
- Lower generator dependency
- Reduced production downtime risk
- More flexibility while load data is collected
- A safer path for staged investment
- Better ability to test the Stage 1 system before committing to full disconnection

## When Going Off-Grid May Make Sense

Going off-grid may make sense later if:

- Grid supply charges become uneconomic.
- Solar and battery performance data proves reliable.
- Workshop loads can be managed without unacceptable downtime.
- Generator runtime is low and predictable.
- Battery autonomy is adequate through poor weather.
- Future loads are known and included in the design.
- Insurance, compliance, safety, and maintenance requirements are acceptable.

## Generator Dependency Risk

Off-grid systems often shift risk from the grid to the generator.

Key risks:

- Fuel cost and fuel storage
- Maintenance and servicing
- Noise and exhaust
- Generator failure during poor weather
- Manual operation burden
- Machine start-up loads exceeding generator capability

Generator integration is useful. Generator dependence should not be the base strategy unless the numbers and operations support it.

## Battery Autonomy Limits

A 100 kWh battery does not mean 100 kWh of fully usable operational capacity in every situation.

Battery autonomy depends on:

- Usable depth of discharge
- Inverter capacity
- Critical load size
- Weather
- Battery reserve settings
- Future accommodation and workshop loads
- Whether EV charging or pumps are allowed during low-solar periods

Autonomy should be modelled after interval data is collected.

## Production Downtime Risk

Workshop downtime may cost more than the grid bill being avoided.

Before considering off-grid operation, the site needs to know:

- Which machines are critical
- Which loads can be shed
- What downtime costs
- How long the site can operate in poor weather
- Whether generator backup can support production safely

## Demand Growth Risk

Future loads may include dam pumping, irrigation, EV charging, accommodation, future buildings, and tiny home manufacturing. Disconnecting too early may force expensive redesign if demand grows faster than expected.

## Recommendation

Build microgrid-capable infrastructure now. Stay grid-connected initially.

Disconnection should only be considered after the site has real operating data from the solar, battery, workshop, and generator system. The decision should be based on measured performance, not expected nameplate capacity.
